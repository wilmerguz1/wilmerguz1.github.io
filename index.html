Introduction¶

When it comes to life, it's very easy to just live in the moment and enjoy it to the best of our abilities. We want to achieve the best version of ourselves which can come in many different forms such as being happy, healthy, and successful, to name a few. Though unfortunately, as the saying goes "there's only 3 things that are certain in life and those are birth, death, and taxes". It's obvious that taxes are the clear outlier here and not relevant for our research, but if you're reading this then you already passed the certainty of birth. That brings us to the last certainty, death. All living things have had a start date and have had or will have an end date. Death is unavoidable, whether we believe in life after death or other religious beliefs, our bodies will stop functioning one day. So I thought it would be useful and interesting to analyze the top 10 leading causes of death in the United States from 1999 to 2017. I also want to look at any unusual changes in the graphs and explore certain outliers. This will allow us to gain a better understanding on how you could possibly expand your life expectancy by keeping an eye out for these unfortunate causes. My main goal here is for people to get the most out of their lives by living it to the fullest whilst also the longest. As a bonus, I want to compare the leading causes of death between Maryland and Missouri because they have an extremely similar population between 1999 and 2017 according to the United States Census Bureau (2021).

Part 2: Data Management¶

I want to look at the first cause name column which is just a more specific/professional name of the type of cause you would hear from a doctor for example, while the second cause name column is what you would most commonly refer the cause to in the simplest terms. The age-adjusted death rate column is just a way to control the differences in the population age distribtuions. So for my purposes I can get rid of the first cause name column because it is just an extension for the second cause name column, as well as the age-adjusted death rate column because I will not be focusing on that in my research. I also want to only look at the data for just the United States throughout the years, so I will also drop all the rows that are not the United States which include the 50 individual states and the District of Columbia. Similarly, I will drop the unnecessary columns and rows for the Maryland table as well as the Missouri table.

Part 5: Conclusion¶

After analyzing the total deaths across the United States from 1999 to 2017 that have occured from the top 10, you can see a lot of similarities for the last 10 or so years.
